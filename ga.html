Mindbrush is an app that promotes mental wellbeing among college students by making aspects of it a daily routine.


This project was a result of my General Assembly UX class and won the concept stage of U.Va’s Entrepreneurship Cup (09.2017).

CHALLENGE: create a solution that elevates college students' mental wellbeing. This project takes a preemptive approach to a complex problem. The concept arose out of a desire to reduce stress and increase quality of life for college students. As this was a final project for the General Assembly User Experience Circuit (fall 2017), the project guidelines entailed creating an app that benefits the user's daily life.

ROLE: research, solution ideation, visual and UX design, usability testing. I worked in a team of three who came from back end development and data science backgrounds. Our research focused on finding barriers to taking care of individual wellbeing in college life.

Through conducting research with over 100 U.Va. students, we found that they struggled to make more time for the people that matter to them daily due to the busy nature of college life. Our initial idea aimed to focus on automatically scheduling meet ups between friends to allieviate the pain of finding a time that works with both parties.

The first iteration included a feature that compares trends between wellbeing and other factors, but we decided to omit this feature due to the subjective nature of measuring inidvidual wellbeing and lack of access to mobile sensing technology.

SOLUTION: this was only the first iteration for this concept and we continued to conduct research to help us address users' pain points. After narrowing the intial iteration's features down to simplify our process, we developed a web application for beta testing.

PROCESS

The inspiration for this project came from our interest in addressing the mental illness problem that plagues college campuses. 41% of college students reported suffering from a mental health issue (National Alliance on Mental Illness, 2016); the other 59% aren’t necessarily “safe” as mental health can affect any person like how physical health can affect anyone. Since mental health statistics depend on individuals self-reporting their personal issues, these figures are likely to be an understatement.

RESEARCH: To figure out how student are currently dealing with mental health issues and what problems they actually face, we conducted over 100 interviews with various U.Va. students. Some of the reoccurring pain points are below.

USER PERSONA: after synthesizing our research, I created a user persona, named Kate, to help our team keep users like her in mind throughout our process in order to avoid making assumptions or jump to conclusions.

MEET KATE

Kate is stressed from knowing what she wants to do in life. On top of this, she has a hard time balancing academics, friends, and self-care. While she values time with friends, she struggles to see them as often as she would like due to difficulty finding time that works for both parties. If she spends long periods of time alone, which is often the case before midterms when she has the added stress of completing all her responsibilities in addition to studying for exams, she feels more unmotivated to do her work. Her lack of motivation doesn’t help with her habits of procrastinating making her more stressed during midterms, because she has less time to stay on top of her responsibilities. This time crunch further makes it difficult for her to remember and prioritize taking care of her physical and mental health during busy times of the school year.


Kate needs an easier way to make more time for the people she cares about during her busy college life, because this will help her stay motivated and increase her mood during stressful times like midterms in particular.

SOLUTION

Many mental health apps currently have low user retention rates as these apps require frequent user input. Further, mental illness is highly stigmatized and most solutions are prescriptive rather than proactive. These apps are targeted to those who suffer from anxiety, depression or some other “mental illness.” In attempts to reduce this stigma, we opted to use the terminology “mental wellbeing" to elevate how people see mental health, rather than primarily associating the term with mental illness. Mental wellbeing is anything from basic self-care, such as catching up on sleep or meditating, to spending quality time with loved ones; in short, it is anything needed for an individual to function at his or her best.

Given that our interviews showed more people are interested in making more time for their friends and that regular face-to-face interaction reduces the risk of depression by 50% (Oregon Health & Science University 2014), we decided the primary feature of Mindbrush should be an automatic scheduler that prompts users when they have mutual availability with their friends and then asks if he or she would like the app to send an invitation for them. This takes away the frustration and time consuming aspect of scheduling meetings with friends by doing it for the user and allows them to not have to worry about making time for friends while having more mental energy to stay on top of other responsibilities.
